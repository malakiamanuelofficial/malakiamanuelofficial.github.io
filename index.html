Work Placement — Keele University

Research Contributor | Growth and Retention Analysis

Market Sizing and Growth Projection for MyLurah

Overview

How many users will a culturally specific reproductive health app realistically attract, retain, and grow in its first year? Whilst working with MyLurah, a tech startup focused on African feminine reproductive health,this was the priority question that we were looking to address. This case study covers my individual contribution to a 7-person collaborative project: building an evidence-based growth and retention model.

Methodology

Benchmark Research: Triangulated growth and retention data from multiple independent sources. Waitlister aggregated launch data, Business of Apps and Orangesoft industry reports, comparable African digital health platforms (Omomi, Afya Pap, Grace Health), peer-reviewed studies, and regional market analysis.

Projection Modelling: Combined three independent metrics through a compound model:

  • Waitlist size (1,000–1,500) × conversion rate (13–16%) = launch active users
  • Launch active users × 90-day retention (10–15%) = retained user base
  • Retained user base × monthly organic growth (7% compounded) = 12-month projection

Key Findings

1. Defensible launch conversion: 13–16% — Aggregated benchmark data placed the realistic median at 13.8% for health and fitness apps. Launch user estimate: 130–240 active users.

2. Retention is the primary constraint — Reproductive health apps lose 85–90% of users within 90 days. Of 130–240 launch users, only 13–36 would remain after three months.

3. Organic growth is real but modest — Comparable African digital health platforms showed 7–10% monthly growth when actively managed.

4. Honest 12-month projection: 330–610 users — Significantly lower than inflated startup forecasts, but defensible and credible for investor conversations.

Applied Recommendations

  • Prioritise retention over acquisition in year one. 85–90% drop-off means features that increase stickiness (community, onboarding, content) have highest leverage
  • Set investor expectations against benchmarks. Defensible forecasts are important.
  • Use ranges, not point estimates. Single-number projections imply false precision where not guaranteed.
  • Treat retention metrics as product feedback, not just KPIs.

Relevance

This project demonstrates ability to move beyond optimistic assumptions and produce defensible, benchmarked projections. Key transferable skill: translating complex data into actionable strategic recommendations for product teams.

Psychology Project — Keele University

Lead Researcher | Grade: 65 (2:1)

Behavioral Drivers of Student Stress

Overview

As student mental health becomes a primary concern for higher education and EdTech providers, understanding the behavioral drivers of stress is critical. This study investigated how two modifiable behaviors, procrastination and physical activity, predict perceived stress in university students.

Methodology

Participants: 149 university students (aged 18–58)

Data Collection: 15-minute digital survey via Qualtrics with randomized block ordering to mitigate order bias

Validated Scales: Pure Procrastination Scale (PPS), Godin Leisure-Time Exercise Questionnaire (GLTEQ), Perceived Stress Scale-10 (PSS-10)

Ethics: Full approval from Psychology Student Project Ethics Committee (SPEC)

Analysis: Multiple Linear Regression in JASP

Key Findings

The Procrastination Trap: Procrastination was a powerful positive predictor of stress (β=.602, p<.001). It accounted for the majority of the 36.1% total variance explained by the model.

The Exercise Myth: Contrary to popular belief, leisure-time physical activity was not a significant predictor of perceived stress (β=.008, p=.909).

The Self-Regulation Gap: Student stress is driven more by cognitive self-regulation than physiological coping resources.

Applied Recommendations

  • Pivot from wellness to workflow. Digital interventions should prioritize task initiation features over generic fitness tracking.
  • Real-time intervention. Apps should help users manage the anxiety of starting a task (emotion-regulation failure) rather than just managing schedules.
  • Workflow integration. Universities should integrate self-regulation coaching into academic portals.

Relevance

Direct insight for student wellbeing and productivity product design. Prioritize cognitive self-regulation support over general wellness features. The finding that task management is more proximally linked to stress than exercise shapes feature prioritization decisions.

Research Designer | Grade: 65 (2:1)

Evaluating Virtual Reality as a Therapeutic Tool

Overview

Can Virtual Reality do what traditional therapy cannot? This research proposal investigated whether VR Exposure Therapy (VRET) could outperform CBT in treating Agoraphobia. The project required synthesising existing clinical literature, identifying gaps, and designing a full research programme.

Methodology

Literature Synthesis: Independent review of peer-reviewed cyberpsychology and clinical psychology literature

Gap Analysis: Identified three critical gaps: absence of direct comparative studies, underexplored cost-effectiveness, insufficient accounting for individual differences

Experimental Design: Mixed design controlled experiment (between-subjects and within-subjects) with 50 clinically diagnosed participants

Measure Selection: Agoraphobia Scale (AS), Panic and Agoraphobia Scale (PAS), Beck Anxiety Inventory (BAI) at baseline, week 4, and week 8

Ethics: Full BPS compliance including informed consent with 24-hour consideration period

Research Insights

VRET's core advantage is customisation: Unlike in-vivo CBT, VRET allows precise environmental control—adjusting crowd density, noise levels, lighting, and spatial scale.

Presence is the critical variable: Psychological presence (how "real" the VR environment feels) is the primary driver of VRET efficacy, not the technology itself.

The evidence gap is a design problem: Absence of robust comparative studies reflects lack of standardised methodology—highlighting need for replicable experimental frameworks.

Cost is a genuine barrier: VR equipment costs constrain clinical adoption—significant opportunity for health tech products targeting accessible hardware.

Applied Recommendations

  • Design for presence by prioritising immersion quality and environmental realism over feature quantity.
  • Modular environment design by building flexible, configurable virtual spaces rather than fixed linear experiences.
  • Reduce the hardware barrier—design effective experiences for standalone headsets or smartphone-based VR
  • Measure outcomes, integrate validated clinical measures at key intervals.

Relevance

Key insight for immersive experience and health tech design: "presence" is the outcome that matters, not technical sophistication. Products must prioritise immersion quality over feature quantity. Cost barriers shape realistic market positioning.

Research Design — Keele University

Research Designer

Dual Methodology Research Design

Overview

Knowing which research method to reach for, and why, is one of the most fundamental skills a researcher brings to a product team. This project required designing two complete research studies from scratch. One quantitative, one qualitative, each addressing a distinct human behaviour question.

Study 1: Quantitative

Research Question: Does listening to music while studying affect long-term memory retention in university students?

Design: Within-subjects experimental design. Each participant experienced both conditions, controlling for individual differences

Participants: Undergraduate students aged 18–25, recruited via convenience sampling

Additional Measure: Likert-scale questionnaire capturing subjective experience of studying with and without music

Analysis: Repeated measures ANOVA

Study 2: Qualitative

Research Question: How do teenagers feel about bullying in secondary school, and what can their lived experiences reveal about intervention needs?

Design: Phenomenological qualitative design. Chosen because bullying is a subjective, emotionally loaded experience

Participants: Secondary school students aged 11–16 who had experienced or witnessed bullying, recruited via purposive sampling

Data Collection: 30-minute semi-structured interviews, audio-recorded and transcribed verbatim

Analysis: Thematic analysis (Braun & Clarke, 2006)

Ethics: Extensive safeguards including anonymisation via pseudonyms, provision of support helplines post-interview, one-week data withdrawal window

Methodological Insights

Method selection is a values decision: Choosing between quantitative and qualitative methods reflects a fundamental position on what counts as knowledge. In UXR, this is made every time a researcher chooses between an A/B test and a user interview.

Within-subjects designs are powerful but ethically complex: They eliminate many confounds but introduce order effects and demand characteristics. Counterbalancing was a necessary design safeguard.

Sensitive topic research demands proactive ethical design: Research with vulnerable or emotionally engaged participants requires additional layers of care that cannot be retrofitted after data collection has begun.

Subjective and objective measures can coexist productively: How users feel about their performance is often as important as their actual performance—a direct parallel to UXR studies where task completion rates are supplemented by satisfaction ratings.

Applied Recommendations

  • Match method to question before selecting tools. Quantitative answers "how much," qualitative answers "why"
  • Design exclusion criteria deliberately, poorly defined exclusion criteria introduce confounds that make findings uninterpretable
  • Build ethical safeguards in at the design stage that ensure safety, validity, and reliability
  • Capture both behaviour and perception. Design studies that capture both provide richer, more actionable insights

Relevance

This project demonstrates my methodological fluency. The ability to think in both quantitative and qualitative modes. A key differentiator in research, matching method to question rather than reaching for familiar tools.

Science Communication — Keele University

Science Communicator / Psychology Writer

Communicating Science to a Public Audience

Overview

Research findings only create value when they reach the people who need them. This project sits at the intersection of psychological research and public communication.The task was to write a critical psychology blog communicating peer-reviewed research on romantic secrecy for general audiences.

Methodology

Audience Definition: Primary audience was explicitly defined as casual readers with no psychology background. Required fundamental reorientation from academic writing conventions

Content Strategy: Narrative hook, problem framing, evidence, and conclusion format—mirroring effective science journalism

Tone Calibration: Writing voice that utilised humour to make uncomfortable findings more approachable

Iterative Refinement: Developed through iterative process informed by feedback, personal reflection, and peer workshop discussion. The 400-word reflective component required explicit metacognitive analysis of communication design decisions.

Key Insights

Framing determines engagement: The same research findings can land very differently depending on how they are introduced. Leading with a relatable human scenario before introducing the research created an emotional hook that motivated continued reading.

Humour is a legitimate communication tool: Strategic wit maintained reader engagement during sections that could otherwise feel dry or lecture-like. This mirrors UX writing principles around voice and tone.

Accuracy and accessibility are not mutually exclusive: With careful word choice and structural discipline, complex psychological findings can be communicated accurately within a 600-word conversational format.

Reflection accelerates skill development: Articulating why specific choices were made revealed that the most significant communication growth came from metacognition, not just writing practice.

Applied Recommendations

  • Lead with the human story, not the data. People engage with findings when they can see themselves in the research
  • Tone is a design decision. The register in which research is communicated shapes how credible and actionable findings feel
  • Constraints improve communication, word limits and time-boxed presentations force prioritisation of the most important insights
  • Build reflection into your process. Making a brief retrospective after each deliverable accelerates skill development

Relevance

Direct UX writing skill: translating complex findings into compelling, accessible narratives for non-expert stakeholders. The ability to balance scientific credibility with public readability is central to effective research communication.

Qualitative Research Methods — Keele University

Qualitative Researcher

Lived Experiences of Cultural Assimilation and Identity

Overview

How does relocating to a foreign country reshape who you are? This qualitative study explored the lived experiences of international students navigating cultural assimilation and personal identity. Using pre-existing interview data from three Keele University international students and alumni, the research applied Reflexive Thematic Analysis to surface the psychological and social processes underlying cultural transition.

Methodology

Philosophical Approach: Experiential epistemological stance. Recognising that human experience is inherently subjective and that understanding lived realities requires immersion in individual narratives rather than reduction to numbers

Data Collection: Pre-existing interview transcripts from 3 participants representing diverse national and linguistic backgrounds

Analytic Approach: Following Braun and Clarke's (2006) six-stage framework: familiarisation, initial coding, theme generation, theme review, theme definition, and write-up

Reflexivity: Maintained a reflexive diary throughout the analytic process, actively monitoring how personal assumptions shaped interpretation of the data

Themes Generated

Theme 1: Identity as Fluid and International

Participants did not describe identity as fixed. Participant (1) : "I don't really have an identity. I don't see myself as being from one country or another. I see myself as being quite international." Identity becomes accumulative and adaptive for individuals with cross-cultural experience.

Theme 2: Assimilation is Not Universal, Intersectionality Matters

Language emerged as a critical intersectional variable. Participant (2) noted shared language significantly eased her transition from America to the UK. Yet she experienced reverse culture shock upon returning home. Assimilation is bidirectional and cumulative.

Theme 3: Motivation Shapes the Depth of Integration

Participant (3)'s primary motivation for living abroad was pragmatic: "I wouldn't say it's the culture. It is just the opportunities." The depth of cultural assimilation is moderated by underlying migration motivation.

Applied Recommendations

  • Abandon the "universal user" assumption. Assimilation experiences differ significantly based on language, cultural distance, and personal motivation
  • Design for transition, not just arrival. Users oscillate between cultural contexts, products should acknowledge ongoing cultural negotiation
  • Motivation-aware personalisation. Users with instrumental motivations engage differently than those with intrinsic motivations
  • Qualitative methods are essential, standardised surveys cannot surface the nuanced, intersectional factors that shape assimilation

Relevance

Key insight for globally diverse or multicultural user base design: assuming a fixed cultural identity as a design variable is an oversimplification. Onboarding and transition experiences must account for individual starting points, not assume a homogeneous user baseline.
